# Required Minimum Distributions

Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) are the IRS's way of ensuring that tax-deferred retirement accounts eventually get taxed. Once you reach a certain age, you must withdraw a minimum amount each year from Traditional IRAs, 401(k)s, 403(b)s, and other tax-deferred accounts — whether you need the money or not.

RMDs are not optional. The penalty for missing an RMD is 25% of the amount you should have withdrawn (reduced from the previous 50% by SECURE Act 2.0). If corrected within two years, the penalty drops to 10%.

## SECURE Act 2.0: Current Rules

The SECURE Act (2019) and SECURE Act 2.0 (2022) significantly changed RMD rules:

| Birth Year | RMD Starting Age |
|-----------|------------------|
| Before 1951 | 70.5 (old rule) |
| 1951-1959 | 73 |
| 1960 or later | 75 |

**Roth IRAs**: No RMDs during the owner's lifetime. This is one of the most powerful advantages of Roth accounts and a key reason for Roth conversions (see [Roth Conversion Strategy](RothConversionStrategy)).

**Roth 401(k)s**: Starting in 2024, Roth 401(k)s are also exempt from RMDs during the owner's lifetime (another SECURE Act 2.0 change). Previously, Roth 401(k)s required RMDs.

## How RMDs Are Calculated

RMD = Account balance (December 31 of prior year) / Life expectancy factor (from IRS Uniform Lifetime Table)

### IRS Uniform Lifetime Table (Selected Ages)

| Age | Distribution Period | RMD % of Balance | RMD on $1M | RMD on $2M |
|-----|-------------------|-----------------|-----------|------------|
| 73 | 26.5 | 3.77% | $37,700 | $75,400 |
| 75 | 24.6 | 4.07% | $40,700 | $81,400 |
| 78 | 21.8 | 4.59% | $45,900 | $91,800 |
| 80 | 20.2 | 4.95% | $49,500 | $99,000 |
| 83 | 17.7 | 5.65% | $56,500 | $113,000 |
| 85 | 16.0 | 6.25% | $62,500 | $125,000 |
| 88 | 13.6 | 7.35% | $73,500 | $147,000 |
| 90 | 12.2 | 8.20% | $82,000 | $164,000 |
| 93 | 10.2 | 9.80% | $98,000 | $196,000 |
| 95 | 8.9 | 11.24% | $112,400 | $224,800 |

### The RMD Tax Bomb

The numbers above reveal the problem. A retiree with $2M in Traditional IRA accounts at age 73 faces a $75,400 forced distribution — on top of Social Security. Combined:

| Income Source | Amount |
|--------------|--------|
| Social Security (couple) | $50,000 |
| RMD | $75,400 |
| **Total taxable income** | **~$125,400** |
| Minus standard deduction | -$30,000 |
| **Taxable income** | **~$95,400** |
| **Tax bracket** | **22%** (at the top of the 12% bracket) |

And the problem worsens every year because RMD percentages increase with age, even if the account balance stays flat. At 85, the same $2M IRA forces $125,000 in distributions.

If this couple had converted $500,000 to Roth over the decade before RMDs began, their Traditional IRA would be $1.5M, the RMD would be $56,600, and their total taxable income would stay in the 12% bracket. The Roth conversions would have cost ~12% tax. The RMDs they avoided would have been taxed at 22%. Net savings: tens of thousands of dollars.

## Strategies to Reduce RMD Impact

### 1. Roth Conversions Before Age 73

The most powerful RMD mitigation strategy. Convert Traditional IRA money to Roth during the gap years between retirement and RMD age. Every dollar converted reduces future RMDs.

**Target**: Reduce your Traditional IRA balance enough that RMDs plus Social Security keep you in the 12% bracket or below.

See [Roth Conversion Strategy](RothConversionStrategy) for detailed conversion planning.

### 2. Qualified Charitable Distributions (QCDs)

After age 70.5, you can direct up to $105,000 per year (2024, indexed for inflation) from your Traditional IRA directly to a qualified charity. QCDs:

- Satisfy your RMD obligation
- Are excluded from taxable income (unlike a normal withdrawal followed by a charitable deduction)
- Reduce your AGI, which can lower IRMAA, reduce Social Security taxation, and keep you in a lower bracket

**Who benefits most**: Retirees who give to charity regularly and whose RMDs exceed their spending needs. Instead of taking the RMD, paying tax, and donating after-tax dollars, the QCD accomplishes all three in one tax-free step.

**Note**: QCDs cannot go to donor-advised funds or private foundations.

### 3. Delay Social Security to Reduce Portfolio Pressure

Smaller portfolio withdrawals in early retirement (because Social Security covers more expenses) mean a larger portfolio at RMD age — which seems counterproductive. But the guaranteed Social Security income means you can afford a higher withdrawal rate from the portfolio *or* more aggressive Roth conversions.

The real benefit: a larger Social Security benefit reduces the *portfolio withdrawal rate* needed, making the overall plan more resilient. See [Social Security Claiming Strategy](SocialSecurityClaimingStrategy).

### 4. Contribute to Roth 401(k) in Later Working Years

If you're still working in your 50s and 60s and expect large RMDs, redirect future contributions to Roth 401(k) instead of Traditional. You pay tax now (while earning) but avoid RMDs on those contributions.

### 5. Use the First RMD Year Wisely

Your first RMD can be deferred until April 1 of the year *after* you turn 73. But if you defer, you'll take two RMDs in the same calendar year (the deferred one plus the current year's), potentially pushing into a higher bracket.

**Usually better**: Take your first RMD in the year you turn 73 to avoid the double-RMD problem.

## Inherited Account Rules (SECURE Act)

The SECURE Act dramatically changed inherited retirement account rules. For most non-spouse beneficiaries who inherit after 2019:

### The 10-Year Rule

- The entire inherited account must be withdrawn within 10 years of the original owner's death
- No annual RMDs required (though the IRS has issued confusing guidance on this for some beneficiaries)
- The 10-year deadline is a hard cutoff — the balance must be zero by December 31 of the 10th year

### Exceptions to the 10-Year Rule

| Beneficiary | Rule |
|-------------|------|
| Surviving spouse | Can roll into own IRA; take RMDs based on own life expectancy |
| Minor children of the deceased | 10-year clock starts when child reaches majority (usually 21) |
| Disabled or chronically ill | Stretch over life expectancy (old rules) |
| Beneficiary not more than 10 years younger than deceased | Stretch over life expectancy |

### Planning Implications for Heirs

If you're leaving a large Traditional IRA to non-spouse heirs (children), they will owe income tax on the entire balance within 10 years. Depending on their own income, this could push them into the 32% or 37% bracket.

**This is another argument for Roth conversions**: Roth IRAs inherited by non-spouse beneficiaries still follow the 10-year rule, but withdrawals are tax-free. Converting to Roth during your low-tax retirement years and paying 12% tax is far better than your heirs paying 32% tax.

## RMD Planning Checklist

1. **Project your Traditional IRA balance at age 73.** Use 6-7% annual growth to estimate.
2. **Calculate expected RMDs.** Use the table above. Add Social Security.
3. **Determine your projected tax bracket.** Is it higher than your current bracket?
4. **If yes, start Roth conversions now.** Target a Traditional IRA balance where RMDs keep you in the 12% bracket.
5. **If you're charitably inclined, plan for QCDs** starting at 70.5.
6. **Consider your heirs.** Will they inherit a large Traditional IRA at high tax rates?
7. **Take your first RMD on time.** Don't defer to the next year unless you model the double-RMD impact.
