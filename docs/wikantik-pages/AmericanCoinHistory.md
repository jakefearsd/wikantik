# American Coin History: The Evolution of Currency

The history of American coinage is a narrative of national identity, [economic expansion](EconomicHistory), and [industrial design](ArtHistory). From the establishment of the first U.S. Mint in 1792 to the high-tech [anti-counterfeiting](ArtHistory) measures of 2026, the physical form of American money has evolved from artisanal precious metal discs into sophisticated industrial instruments.

## 1. Technical Eras of American Coinage

### 1.1 The Classical Silver Era (1792–1836)
Early U.S. coins were struck using manual screw presses and artisanal dies. The metallurgical standard was **90% Silver and 10% Copper**, a ratio chosen for its balance of intrinsic value and durability.
*   **Key Designs**: Flowing Hair and Draped Bust series.

### 1.2 The Gobrecht Renaissance (1836–1891)
Chief Engraver **Christian Gobrecht** introduced mechanical precision to the Mint. Using his **medal-ruling machine**, he standardized the **Seated Liberty** design across nearly all silver denominations (Dime, Quarter, Half Dollar, and Dollar), creating the first cohesive "look" for American currency.

### 1.3 The "Golden Age" of Coinage (1907–1921)
Driven by President Theodore Roosevelt's desire for a "Renaissance" of American design, this era saw the hiring of outside sculptors like **Augustus Saint-Gaudens** and **Adolph A. Weinman**.
*   **Saint-Gaudens Double Eagle (1907)**: Widely considered the most beautiful U.S. coin. Its transition from Ultra-High Relief to a circulating version epitomizes the clash between [Fine Art](ArtHistory) and [Industrial Mintability](ArtHistory).
*   **The Morgan Dollar (1878–1921)**: Designed by George T. Morgan, this coin utilized high-relief, realistic American portraits, becoming the most collected silver coin in history.

## 2. The Great Transition: From Silver to Fiat
The 20th century saw the metallurgical "hollowing out" of American coinage as it moved from intrinsic to token value.

| Date | Event | Metallurgical Change |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1933** | Gold Recall | End of circulating gold coinage. |
| **1964** | Last 90% Silver | Final year of high-purity silver for general circulation. |
| **1965** | Coinage Act | Transition to Cupro-Nickel "Clad" for Quarters and Dimes. |
| **1971** | Nixon Shock | End of [Bretton Woods](EconomicHistory); coins become pure fiat tokens. |
| **1982** | Copper-to-Zinc | The Penny composition shifts to 97.5% Zinc for cost efficiency. |

## 3. 2026: The Semiquincentennial
In 2026, the U.S. Mint is releasing special commemorative designs for the **250th Anniversary (Semiquincentennial)**. These issues utilize modern **laser-frosted** dies and high-precision striking tech, targeting a new generation of "investor-collectors."
