# Annuity Types and Analysis

The annuity market is large and confusing. Many products; many fee structures; many marketing claims. Most products are overpriced or unnecessary; a few are genuinely useful.

This page covers the major types and which ones are worth considering.

## SPIA (Single-Premium Immediate Annuity)

The simplest annuity. Pay lump sum; receive monthly income starting immediately, for life or a defined period.

**How it works**: insurance company combines your money with thousands of other annuitants. People who die early subsidize people who live long (mortality credits). Insurer profits modestly.

**Pros**:
- Transparent
- Low fees
- Predictable income
- Strong longevity protection

**Cons**:
- No liquidity (lump sum gone)
- Fixed nominal payment (no inflation adjustment unless you buy that rider)
- Counterparty risk
- No remainder for heirs (in life-only)

**When it fits**: filling the income floor in retirement when guaranteed income (Social Security, pensions) is insufficient.

**Best practice**: shop multiple insurers; pick A+ rated; consider laddering purchases over multiple years.

## Deferred Income Annuity (DIA) / QLAC

Like SPIA but income starts later — say, age 80 or 85. Cheaper because the deferral period grows the balance.

**Qualified Longevity Annuity Contract (QLAC)**: a special version using IRA money. Up to ~$200K can be purchased; doesn't count toward RMDs until income starts.

**When it fits**: as longevity insurance. Buy at 65 with income starting at 85. Provides a safety net if you live unusually long.

**Best practice**: small allocation (10-15% of retirement assets max). Don't over-rely; keep most assets liquid for the ages 65-85 period.

## Fixed Annuity

Insurance company guarantees a fixed interest rate for a period (typically 3-10 years). Like a CD with annuity wrapper.

**Pros**:
- Guaranteed return
- Tax-deferred growth
- Predictable

**Cons**:
- Surrender charges if accessed early
- Often lower returns than CDs
- Tax-deferred only matters if not in IRA
- Locked in if rates rise

**When it fits**: rarely. CDs or short-duration bonds usually do the same job better.

## Multi-Year Guaranteed Annuity (MYGA)

A specific fixed annuity with a multi-year rate guarantee.

**When it fits**: occasional cases where the rate beats CDs and the lockup is acceptable. Compare carefully to alternatives.

## Variable Annuity

Sub-account investments (like mutual funds) inside an annuity wrapper. Returns vary with the markets.

**Fees**: notoriously high. Mortality and expense charges (M&E) typically 1-1.5% annually. Plus sub-account fees (often another 1%). Plus rider fees if you add guarantees.

Total annual cost: 2-3.5% is common. This is dramatic drag.

**Pros**:
- Tax-deferred growth (in non-IRA contexts)
- Can include guaranteed income riders

**Cons**:
- Very high fees
- Surrender charges (often 7+ years)
- Complex
- Most retail-sold versions are bad value

**When it fits**: rare. The "buy term and invest the difference" logic of life insurance applies — invest in low-cost mutual funds; protect against longevity with a SPIA if needed.

**Best practice**: avoid retail-sold variable annuities. The few that make sense are low-cost institutional versions (Vanguard, Fidelity) for very specific tax situations.

## Indexed Annuity / Equity-Indexed Annuity (EIA) / Fixed Indexed Annuity (FIA)

The most-marketed; the most-problematic.

Pitched as "stock market upside without downside risk." The reality: returns capped (often 4-6% annually, sometimes much less); participation rates change after purchase; surrender charges are aggressive (often 10+ years).

**Pros (advertised)**: market gains without losses.

**Cons (reality)**:
- Caps and participation rates limit upside dramatically
- Insurer can change crediting parameters
- Long surrender periods
- Complex; marketing oversimplifies

**When it fits**: almost never. The "no downside" pitch sounds appealing; the small print eliminates most upside.

**Best practice**: avoid. If you want some equity exposure with downside protection, structured products from large banks (with full disclosure) or simply a balanced portfolio with bonds usually work better.

## Annuity riders

Most annuities can have riders attached:

- **Guaranteed Lifetime Withdrawal Benefit (GLWB)**: minimum withdrawal regardless of account balance
- **Guaranteed Minimum Income Benefit (GMIB)**: option to annuitize at minimum rate
- **Death benefit rider**: increases the death benefit

Each rider adds a fee. Math the fee against the value of the guarantee. Often the fees compound to make the guarantee uneconomic.

## How to evaluate an annuity

When presented with an annuity:

1. **What's the actual annual cost?** All fees, totaled.
2. **What's the surrender period and schedule?** If you change your mind in year 3, what's the cost?
3. **What's the alternative?** What would the same money do in a portfolio?
4. **What's the commission to the seller?** This drives a lot of recommendations.
5. **Is the company A+ rated?** AM Best, Standard & Poor's.

## A reasonable position

For most retirees considering annuities:

- **SPIA**: yes, possibly. For filling the income floor.
- **DIA / QLAC**: yes, possibly. For longevity insurance.
- **Fixed annuity / MYGA**: maybe, sometimes. CDs usually do as well.
- **Variable annuity**: no, except in narrow cases.
- **Indexed annuity**: no.

The annuity industry sells products that aren't always in clients' interests. SPIAs are the workhorse; the others usually aren't worth it.

See [AnnuitiesVsSystematicWithdrawals](AnnuitiesVsSystematicWithdrawals) for the comparison vs. portfolio withdrawal.

## Common failure patterns

- **Variable annuity sold as "investment"**: it's expensive insurance; usually wrong tool.
- **Indexed annuity for "safe upside"**: caps prevent meaningful upside.
- **Annuitizing too much**: no liquidity; no inflation hedge.
- **Buying without comparing alternatives**: maybe a SPIA; maybe just keep investing.
- **Ignoring the commission incentive**: agents push high-commission products.
