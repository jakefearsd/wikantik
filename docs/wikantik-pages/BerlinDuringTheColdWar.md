# Berlin During the Cold War: 1945–1990

For more than four decades, **Berlin during the Cold War** was the most concentrated flashpoint of the global standoff between the United States and the Soviet Union. A single city — occupied by four powers, split by a wall, and ringed by hostile territory — became the place where the Iron Curtain was most visible and where the conflict came closest to open war. This guide traces Berlin's journey from the 1945 four-power occupation, through the 1948 Blockade, the 1953 uprising, the building of the Berlin Wall in 1961, the long division, and finally the fall of the Wall and German reunification in 1990.

> **Quick answer:** Berlin was divided because the four victorious Allies of World War II — the US, UK, France, and the Soviet Union — each occupied a sector of the city, which sat *entirely inside* the Soviet occupation zone of Germany. As the wartime alliance collapsed into the Cold War, that arrangement hardened into a frontier, and in 1961 the East German government walled off West Berlin to stop its citizens from fleeing west.

## 1. Occupation and the Four-Sector City (1945)

Following the surrender of Nazi Germany in May 1945, the **Potsdam Agreement** confirmed the division of both Germany and its capital into four occupation zones. Although Berlin lay roughly 160 km inside the Soviet zone, the city itself was split into four sectors:

- **Soviet Sector:** East Berlin — later the capital of the German Democratic Republic (GDR / East Germany).
- **American, British, and French Sectors:** West Berlin — politically and economically tied to the Federal Republic of Germany (FRG / West Germany), but a geographic island surrounded by GDR territory.

Access to West Berlin depended on three air corridors and a handful of road, rail, and canal routes negotiated with the Soviets — a vulnerability that would be tested almost immediately.

## 2. The Berlin Blockade and Airlift (1948–1949)

On **June 24, 1948**, the Soviet Union cut off all land and water access to West Berlin in response to the Western Allies' introduction of the new Deutsche Mark, an attempt to starve the western sectors into submission and absorb them into the Soviet zone.

- **Operation Vittles:** Rather than abandon the city or force a military convoy through, the Western Allies supplied West Berlin's 2+ million residents entirely by air. Over roughly 15 months, pilots flew about **277,500 flights** carrying some **2.3 million tons** of food, coal, and medicine.
- **Three airfields:** Flights used Tempelhof (US sector) and Gatow (British sector); a third airfield at **Tegel** was built in the French sector in just 90 days to add capacity.
- **The "Candy Bombers":** Pilots such as Gail Halvorsen dropped chocolate and gum on small parachutes to children below — "Operation Little Vittles" — a propaganda and morale triumph for the West.
- **Outcome:** Unable to stop the airlift, the Soviets lifted the blockade on **May 12, 1949**. The episode cemented West Berlin's status as a symbol of Western resolve and accelerated the formal creation of two German states later that year.

## 3. The Uprising of June 17, 1953

A strike by East Berlin construction workers against raised production quotas escalated into a widespread anti-government uprising across the GDR.

- **Suppression:** Soviet tanks and troops crushed the revolt within days.
- **Casualties:** Estimates range from roughly 55 to over 100 deaths, with thousands arrested.
- **Legacy:** West Germany made June 17 a national holiday ("Day of German Unity") until reunification moved the date to October 3.

## 4. The 1961 Berlin Crisis and the Wall

By 1961, the open border in Berlin had become an escape hatch: an estimated 2.5–3 million East Germans — many of them young, skilled workers — had fled west since 1949, a "brain drain" that threatened the GDR's survival.

- **August 13, 1961:** Operation Rose began at midnight. East German forces sealed the border with barbed wire, soon replaced by a concrete barrier. The completed system eventually ran about **155 km** around West Berlin, with a guarded "death strip," watchtowers, and anti-vehicle obstacles.
- **Checkpoint Charlie standoff:** In **October 1961**, US and Soviet tanks faced off for about 16 hours at the Friedrichstraße crossing — one of the tensest direct confrontations of the Cold War.
- **Human cost:** Over the Wall's 28-year existence, **at least 140 people died** at the Berlin Wall, including 91 shot by GDR border guards while trying to escape.

## 5. Life in the Divided City

The Wall did not just separate a city; it split families, transit lines, and a shared culture.

- **East Berlin** became the GDR's showcase capital, with prestige projects like the Fernsehturm (TV Tower) on Alexanderplatz, alongside pervasive Stasi surveillance and consumer shortages.
- **West Berlin** was subsidized by Bonn as a "shop window of the West," exempt from West German conscription — which drew students, artists, and countercultural figures. (David Bowie and Iggy Pop's late-1970s "Berlin period" is the best-known cultural footnote.)
- **Escape attempts:** East Berliners tunneled, swam canals, hid in vehicles, and even used a homemade hot-air balloon. "Tunnel 57" (1964) helped 57 people escape under the border before it was discovered.

## 6. Hallstein Doctrine vs. Ostpolitik (1960s–1970s)

- **Hallstein Doctrine:** West Germany's policy of refusing diplomatic relations with any state (except the USSR) that recognized the GDR — an attempt to isolate East Germany internationally.
- **Ostpolitik:** Chancellor Willy Brandt's policy of engagement, begun in 1969, sought normalization with the East. It produced the **1971 Four Power Agreement (Quadripartite Agreement)** on Berlin, which eased travel and communication and stabilized the city's status, reducing it as a trigger for crisis.

## 7. The Fall of the Wall and Reunification (1989–1990)

- **November 9, 1989:** Amid mass protests and a wave of liberalization across the Eastern Bloc, GDR spokesman **Günter Schabowski** announced — prematurely and ambiguously — that travel restrictions were lifted "immediately, without delay." Crowds surged to the crossings; overwhelmed guards opened the gates, and Berliners began dismantling the Wall by hand.
- **October 3, 1990:** Formal German reunification. The GDR acceded to the Federal Republic, and Berlin was designated the capital of a unified Germany.
- **Aftermath:** A 1991 Bundestag vote returned the seat of government from Bonn to Berlin, with the move largely completed by 1999.

## Key Geopolitical Impacts

- **1949:** Creation of two German states (FRG and GDR) — the division of Berlin foreshadowed the division of Europe.
- **1963:** John F. Kennedy's "Ich bin ein Berliner" speech affirmed Western commitment to West Berlin.
- **1987:** Ronald Reagan's "Tear down this wall!" at the Brandenburg Gate became an emblem of the Cold War's final phase.
- **1989–90:** The fall of the Wall became the defining image of the collapse of communism in Europe.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Why was Berlin divided during the Cold War?**
Berlin was jointly occupied by the four WWII Allies but sat inside the Soviet zone. As the alliance broke down, the western and Soviet sectors aligned with opposing blocs, and in 1961 East Germany walled off West Berlin to stop emigration.

**When was the Berlin Wall built, and when did it fall?**
Construction began on August 13, 1961. The Wall fell on November 9, 1989.

**How many people died at the Berlin Wall?**
At least 140 people died at the Berlin Wall between 1961 and 1989, including 91 shot by GDR border guards.

**What was the Berlin Airlift?**
After the Soviets blockaded West Berlin in 1948, the Western Allies supplied the city entirely by air — about 277,500 flights carrying 2.3 million tons of goods — until the blockade was lifted in May 1949.

**When did Germany reunify?**
Formal reunification took place on October 3, 1990, with Berlin designated the capital of a unified Germany.
