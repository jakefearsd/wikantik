# Berlin's Transformation from Margraviate to Capital City

The story of how Berlin evolved from a modest medieval trading settlement on the River Spree into the capital of one of Europe's great powers spans more than six centuries. This transformation was not inevitable — Berlin was never a Roman city, never a major religious seat, and for much of the medieval period was overshadowed by older, wealthier German cities. Its rise was driven by the ambitions of the Hohenzollern dynasty, strategic geography, calculated policies of religious tolerance, and the broader political currents that shaped Central Europe.

## The Founding of Berlin-Cölln (1230s)

Berlin originated as twin settlements: Cölln, on an island in the River Spree (first documented in 1237), and [Berlin](Berlin), on the river's eastern bank (documented in 1244). Both were likely founded by German settlers during the eastward colonization movement (Ostsiedlung) that brought Germanic peoples into Slavic territories in the 12th and 13th centuries. The area had been home to Slavic peoples, particularly the Hevelli and Sprevane tribes, for centuries before German settlement.

The settlements sat at a natural crossing point of the Spree, on trade routes connecting the Elbe region to the west with the Oder region to the east. This location, while not as advantageous as that of cities on major rivers like the Rhine, provided enough commercial activity to sustain growth. By the late 13th century, Berlin-Cölln had joined the Hanseatic League, participating in the trading network that linked cities across northern Europe.

In 1307, the two towns merged their administrations while retaining separate councils, a practical arrangement for managing shared infrastructure like bridges and markets.

## The Margraviate of Brandenburg

Berlin's fortunes were tied to the Margraviate of Brandenburg, one of the seven electorates of the Holy Roman Empire — meaning its ruler was one of the princes who elected the Emperor. The margraviate was established in 1157 by Albert the Bear, and its political importance exceeded its economic weight. For much of the medieval period, Brandenburg was a sparsely populated frontier territory, less prosperous than the Rhineland, Bavaria, or Saxony.

Berlin-Cölln served as one of several towns in the margraviate, not yet a capital in any formal sense. The margraves of the Ascanian dynasty, and later the Wittelsbach and Luxembourg dynasties, moved between various residences. Brandenburg an der Havel and Tangermünde both served as seats of government before Berlin.

## The Hohenzollern Dynasty's Rise

The pivotal moment in Berlin's history came in 1415, when Frederick of Hohenzollern, Burgrave of Nuremberg, was appointed Elector of Brandenburg by Emperor Sigismund as a reward for political and financial support. Frederick I arrived in Brandenburg in 1412 and made Berlin-Cölln his primary residence, beginning the Hohenzollern dynasty's association with the city that would last until 1918.

The Hohenzollerns were initially unpopular. Berlin's merchant class, accustomed to considerable autonomy, resisted the new rulers. In 1442-1448, Elector Frederick II (called "Iron Tooth") crushed the towns' independence in what became known as the "Berlin Indignation" (Berliner Unwille). He confiscated town property, revoked Berlin-Cölln's membership in the Hanseatic League, and began construction of a permanent electoral palace on Cölln island. From this point, Berlin's development was inseparable from the interests of the Hohenzollern dynasty.

By 1451, Berlin-Cölln was declared the official residence city (Residenzstadt) of the Electors of Brandenburg, giving it a status that made it the de facto capital even without a formal proclamation.

## The Thirty Years' War: Devastation

The Thirty Years' War (1618-1648) brought catastrophe to Brandenburg and Berlin. Though the fighting did not reach Berlin in its earliest years, the later stages of the war were devastating. Swedish and Imperial troops occupied and plundered the region repeatedly. Berlin's population, which had reached approximately 12,000 before the war, was roughly halved by war, plague, and famine. Brandenburg as a whole lost an estimated one-third to one-half of its population.

The Treaty of Westphalia (1648) ended the war but left Brandenburg impoverished and depopulated. Berlin was a battered, provincial town — a striking contrast to the vibrant capital it would become within two generations.

## Frederick William, the Great Elector (1640-1688)

The rebuilding of Berlin and Brandenburg was the achievement of Frederick William, known as the Great Elector, who ruled from 1640 to 1688. He inherited a devastated territory and transformed it into one of the significant powers of northern Europe.

### Military and Political Consolidation

Frederick William built a standing army — unusual for a German state of Brandenburg's size — and used it to secure his territories. He gained sovereignty over the Duchy of Prussia (in what is now northeastern Poland) through the Treaties of Wehlau and Oliva (1657-1660), creating the composite state of Brandenburg-Prussia.

### The Edict of Potsdam (1685) and Huguenot Immigration

Perhaps the Great Elector's most consequential decision for Berlin was the 1685 Edict of Potsdam, issued in response to Louis XIV's revocation of the Edict of Nantes, which had protected French Protestants (Huguenots). Frederick William invited Huguenot refugees to settle in Brandenburg, offering them land, tax exemptions, and religious freedom.

Approximately 20,000 Huguenots came to Brandenburg, with roughly 6,000 settling in Berlin. By 1700, one-fifth of Berlin's population was of French origin. The Huguenots brought skills in weaving, dyeing, glassmaking, goldsmithing, and clockmaking, along with commercial networks and cultural sophistication. They established French-language churches, schools, and newspapers, creating a cosmopolitan atmosphere that distinguished Berlin from other German cities. For a broader examination of how religious change shaped Berlin's development, see [Reformation and Urban Development in Berlin](ReformationAndUrbanDevelopmentInBerlin).

### Urban Expansion

Frederick William oversaw the first major expansion of Berlin beyond its medieval core. He ordered the construction of Dorotheenstadt (named for his second wife) and Friedrichswerder as new planned districts. The Unter den Linden boulevard, originally a bridle path to the royal hunting grounds in the Tiergarten, was developed into a tree-lined avenue. Fortifications were modernized, and the first street lighting was installed.

By the Great Elector's death in 1688, Berlin's population had grown to approximately 20,000 — still modest by European standards, but a remarkable recovery from the devastation of the Thirty Years' War.

## Frederick I: Royal Capital of Prussia (1701)

Frederick William's son, Elector Frederick III, had ambitions that extended beyond the title of Elector. Through diplomatic maneuvering (and a substantial payment to the Holy Roman Emperor), he secured permission to crown himself King in Prussia. The coronation took place on January 18, 1701, in Königsberg, and Frederick III became King Frederick I.

Berlin was now the capital of a kingdom. Frederick I invested heavily in the city's appearance to match its new status:

- **Charlottenburg Palace:** Originally built as a summer residence for Queen Sophie Charlotte, it was expanded into a grand baroque palace.
- **The Berlin Academy of Sciences** (Sozietät der Wissenschaften): Founded in 1700 on the advice of Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz.
- **The Arsenal (Zeughaus):** Completed on Unter den Linden, now the German Historical Museum.
- **Urban unification:** In 1709, the five previously separate towns of Berlin, Cölln, Friedrichswerder, Dorotheenstadt, and Friedrichstadt were merged into a single royal capital city.

### Cultural Aspirations

Frederick I and Queen Sophie Charlotte cultivated court culture on the French model. Sophie Charlotte, herself a notable intellectual who corresponded with Leibniz, made the court a gathering place for philosophers, musicians, and scientists. This cultural investment, while expensive, established Berlin's aspiration to be more than a garrison town.

## Frederick William I: The Soldier King (1713-1740)

Frederick I's son, Frederick William I, was his father's opposite in temperament — austere, militaristic, and suspicious of luxury. He slashed court spending, dismissed artists and intellectuals, and redirected resources to the military. Under his rule, the Prussian army grew to 80,000 men, the fourth-largest in Europe despite Prussia being only the thirteenth-largest state by population.

Despite his austerity, Frederick William I contributed significantly to Berlin's development. He oversaw the expansion of the Friedrichstadt district, established manufacturers and workshops, and imposed the Prussian virtues of order, discipline, and efficiency that would characterize Berlin's administrative culture for centuries. The city's population reached 90,000 by the end of his reign.

## Frederick the Great: Enlightenment Capital (1740-1786)

Frederick II (Frederick the Great) combined his father's military acumen with a passionate commitment to culture and the Enlightenment. He transformed Berlin into a major European cultural center:

- **Architecture:** Frederick commissioned the Forum Fridericianum (today's Bebelplatz), including the Berlin State Opera, St. Hedwig's Cathedral, and the Royal Library. These neoclassical buildings gave Berlin's center a grandeur it had previously lacked.
- **Intellectual life:** Frederick corresponded with Voltaire (who lived at the Berlin court from 1750-1753), patronized the Berlin Academy, and promoted religious tolerance and press freedom (within limits).
- **Sans-Souci Palace:** Built in Potsdam as Frederick's personal retreat, it became a symbol of enlightened absolutism.
- **Legal reform:** Frederick reformed the Prussian legal code, abolished torture (with exceptions), and established a degree of judicial independence.

Berlin's population grew to approximately 150,000 by 1786, making it one of the larger cities in the German-speaking world, though still smaller than Vienna or Hamburg.

## The Path to Imperial Capital (1786-1871)

The decades between Frederick the Great's death and German unification saw Berlin continue to grow and modernize:

- **Napoleonic era:** Prussia's defeat by Napoleon in 1806 led to French occupation of Berlin and a period of humiliation, but also to sweeping reforms (Stein-Hardenberg reforms) that modernized Prussian administration, freed the serfs, and opened the civil service to non-nobles.
- **University of Berlin:** Founded in 1810 by Wilhelm von Humboldt, it became a model for research universities worldwide. Scholars including Hegel, Fichte, and later Helmholtz taught there.
- **Industrialization:** The 1840s brought railroads and factories. Berlin became a center of the electrical industry (Siemens), machinery, and chemical manufacturing. The population surged past 400,000 by 1850 and reached 800,000 by 1870.
- **1848 Revolution:** The March Revolution brought barricade fighting to Berlin's streets. Though the revolution ultimately failed, it demonstrated the political energy of Berlin's growing working and middle classes.

### Unification: Capital of the German Empire (1871)

On January 18, 1871, in the Hall of Mirrors at Versailles, Wilhelm I was proclaimed German Emperor (Kaiser), and the German Empire was established under Prussian leadership. Berlin became the imperial capital, a status that reflected both Prussia's dominance and Berlin's emergence as Germany's largest, most economically dynamic city.

The transformation was complete. A settlement that had begun as a cluster of houses by a river crossing had become the capital of one of Europe's great powers — a journey shaped by dynastic ambition, strategic immigration policy, military power, industrial development, and a persistent aspiration to cultural greatness.
