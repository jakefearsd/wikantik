# The Carnation Revolution: The Day Portugal Woke Up (April 25, 1974)

Shortly after midnight on April 25, 1974, a Portuguese radio station played "Grandola, Vila Morena," a folk song by the protest singer Jose Afonso that had been banned by the regime. It was not an accident. The song was a prearranged signal, telling military conspirators across the country that the revolution had begun.

## The Armed Forces Movement

The revolution was launched by the Movimento das Forcas Armadas (MFA, Armed Forces Movement), an organization of young and middle-ranking military officers, many of them captains who had served in the colonial wars in Africa. Their motivations were not ideological in the conventional sense: they were professional soldiers who had concluded that the wars in Angola, Mozambique, and Guinea-Bissau were unwinnable and that the regime that insisted on fighting them had to go.

The MFA had been organizing secretly since 1973, meeting in private homes and communicating through trusted intermediaries. The movement's chief strategist was Major Otelo Saraiva de Carvalho, who planned the military operations from a command post in Lisbon's Pontinha barracks.

## Twenty-Five Hours That Changed a Nation

The operation unfolded with remarkable precision for what was essentially a conspiracy among junior officers. At 12:25 a.m., the first signal was broadcast on radio. Military units across Portugal began moving according to plan.

By dawn, tanks had surrounded the headquarters of the GNR (National Republican Guard) and the DGS (the secret police, formerly PIDE). Key government buildings and communications centers were seized. Marcelo Caetano, the prime minister, took refuge in the Carmo barracks in central Lisbon. By mid-afternoon, he was surrounded by MFA troops and a growing crowd of civilians.

Caetano agreed to surrender, but only to General Antonio de Spinola, a senior officer he considered sufficiently respectable, rather than to junior captains. Spinola arrived, accepted the surrender, and Caetano was escorted to the airport and flown to exile in Brazil, where he would die in 1980.

The most remarkable aspect of the revolution was how little blood was shed. Four people were killed when DGS agents fired on a crowd outside their headquarters on Rua Antonio Maria Cardoso. Otherwise, the transition was peaceful. Soldiers who had spent years fighting in Africa found themselves embraced by jubilant civilians on the streets of Lisbon.

## The Carnations

The revolution gets its name from a spontaneous act. A restaurant worker named Celeste Caeiro had been preparing carnations for the restaurant's first anniversary celebration. Finding the restaurant closed due to the revolution, she began distributing the flowers to soldiers. Others followed her example. By afternoon, soldiers had carnations stuck in their rifle barrels, in their helmets, and in the muzzles of their tanks. The image became one of the most iconic of the twentieth century: armed revolution made gentle by flowers.

## The PREC: Revolution Within the Revolution

If the overthrow of the regime took twenty-five hours, the struggle over what would replace it lasted nearly two years. The period from April 25, 1974, to November 25, 1975, known as the PREC (Processo Revolucionario Em Curso, Ongoing Revolutionary Process), was one of the most turbulent and fascinating periods in modern European history.

Power was initially held by the Junta de Salvacao Nacional (National Salvation Junta), led by General Spinola. But the MFA's rank and file were more radical than Spinola, and political parties that had been suppressed for decades, from communists to socialists to social democrats, suddenly flooded the public sphere.

The PREC saw waves of revolutionary activity: banks and industries were nationalized, large agricultural estates in the Alentejo region were seized by workers' cooperatives, political prisoners were released from Tarrafal and other prisons, and the PIDE/DGS was dissolved. Political rallies, strikes, and demonstrations became daily occurrences in a country that had known only silence for four decades.

Spinola attempted a conservative counter-coup on September 28, 1974, and was forced to resign. The revolution moved leftward, with the Communist Party (PCP) and radical military officers gaining influence. For several months in 1975, Portugal seemed on the verge of becoming a communist state or descending into civil war.

The crisis was resolved on November 25, 1975, when moderate military officers loyal to Ramalho Eanes moved against radical left-wing units. The moderates prevailed without significant violence, and Portugal's path toward parliamentary democracy was secured.

## Decolonization

The MFA's first priority after overthrowing the regime was ending the colonial wars. Negotiations with independence movements began almost immediately. Guinea-Bissau's independence was recognized in September 1974, having been declared unilaterally in 1973. Mozambique became independent in June 1975 under FRELIMO. Cape Verde and Sao Tome and Principe followed in July 1975. Angola's independence in November 1975 was followed immediately by civil war among rival factions, a conflict that would last until 2002.

The most tragic case was East Timor. Portugal withdrew in 1975 without establishing a stable government, and Indonesia invaded in December 1975, beginning a brutal occupation that lasted until 1999 and cost an estimated 100,000 to 180,000 lives.

Decolonization also meant the return of approximately 800,000 retornados, Portuguese settlers and their descendants from the former colonies, to a country of only nine million people. Their integration, though initially difficult and often traumatic, ultimately enriched Portuguese society and economy.

## A New Constitution and Democracy

The Constitution of 1976 established Portugal as a democratic republic with extensive social rights. It reflected the revolutionary moment, including references to the transition to socialism, though these provisions were gradually moderated through subsequent revisions. The first free elections in decades were held on April 25, 1975, exactly one year after the revolution, with voter turnout exceeding 91 percent.

Mario Soares of the Socialist Party became the dominant political figure of the transition, serving as both prime minister and later president. The consolidation of democracy was completed with Portugal's accession to the European Economic Community in 1986, which anchored the country in the Western democratic order.

## The Legacy of April 25

The Carnation Revolution is commemorated annually as Freedom Day, Portugal's most important national holiday. The revolution demonstrated that authoritarian regimes can be overthrown without civil war, a lesson studied by democratic movements worldwide. The bridge across the Tagus in Lisbon, originally named the Salazar Bridge, was renamed the 25 de Abril Bridge, a daily reminder of the day Portugal chose freedom.
