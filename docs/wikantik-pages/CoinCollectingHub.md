# Numismatic Hub: Historical Eras and Grading Mechanics

This hub serves as the primary technical index for coin collecting, prioritizing the structural classification of historical eras and the rigorous application of grading standards.

## 1. Classification by Historical Eras

### A. Ancient and Classical (700 BC – 500 AD)
Focuses on the origins of coinage in Lydia and the subsequent standardization across the Greek city-states and the Roman Empire.
*   **Key Innovation:** Transition from irregular electrum ingots to standardized weight systems (e.g., the Athenian Tetradrachm).
*   **Reference:** [Ancient Trade Routes and Specie](TradeRoutes).

### B. Medieval and Early Modern (500 AD – 1792)
Characterized by the transition from "hammered" coinage to "milled" (machine-struck) coinage.
*   **Hammered Coinage:** Produced by placing a planchet between two dies and striking the upper die with a hammer. High variance in centration and strike quality.
*   **Milled Coinage:** Introduced in the mid-16th century using screw presses, providing consistent diameter and the ability to add "reeded" or lettered edges to prevent clipping.
*   **Colonial American:** [Early American Coins](EarlyAmericanCoins) (Fugio Cents, Pine Tree Shillings).

### C. The Federal Era (1792 – 1964)
The period of precious metal circulation in the United States, from the Mint Act of 1792 to the removal of silver from circulating coinage in 1965.
*   **Key Transitions:**
    *   **1834:** Reduction in gold content to prevent mass exportation.
    *   **1857:** Abolition of the Half Cent and the transition from Large Cents to Small Cents (Flying Eagle).
    *   **1933:** Executive Order 6102 (Gold Recall), ending the circulation of gold coinage.
*   **Reference:** [American Coin History](AmericanCoinHistory).

### D. The Modern Era (1965 – Present)
The transition to base-metal "clad" coinage and the rise of collector-specific proof and commemorative issues.
*   **Metallurgy:** Copper-nickel clad over a pure copper core.

## 2. Technical Grading Standards

Numismatic value is primarily derived from a coin's state of preservation, measured using the Sheldon Scale.

### The Sheldon Scale (1–70)
| Grade | Descriptor | Technical Requirements |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **P-1** | Poor | Identifiable date and type; mostly smooth. |
| **VG-8** | Very Good | Main features clear; rims intact. |
| **VF-20** | Very Fine | Moderate wear on high points; all lettering sharp. |
| **XF-40** | Extremely Fine | Slight wear on highest points; traces of mint luster. |
| **AU-50** | About Uncirculated | Wear only on the very highest points; 50%+ luster. |
| **MS-60** | Mint State | No traces of wear; may have contact marks/scratches. |
| **MS-65** | Choice Mint State | Strong luster; very few distracting marks. |
| **MS-70** | Perfect | Flawless under 5x magnification. |

### Technical Evaluation Criteria
1.  **Surface Preservation:** Evaluation of "bag marks" (contact marks from other coins in mint bags), hairlines, and environmental spotting.
2.  **Strike Quality:** The degree to which the metal filled the dies. Weak strikes on high-relief issues (e.g., [1916 Standing Liberty Quarters](AmericanCoinHistory)) significantly impact value even in high grades.
3.  **Luster:** The "cartwheel" effect caused by flow lines in the metal during striking. Luster is the first thing lost to wear or improper cleaning.
4.  **Eye Appeal:** A subjective but market-critical assessment of toning, color, and overall balance.
