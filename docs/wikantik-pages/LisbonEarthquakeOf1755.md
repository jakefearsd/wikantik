# The Lisbon Earthquake of 1755: When the Earth Shook Philosophy

On the morning of November 1, 1755, All Saints' Day, the churches of Lisbon were packed with worshippers. At approximately 9:40 a.m., the ground began to shake. What followed was one of the most catastrophic natural disasters in European history, an event that killed tens of thousands, destroyed one of Europe's wealthiest cities, and sent shockwaves through the intellectual foundations of the Enlightenment.

## The Triple Disaster

The earthquake itself lasted between three and a half and six minutes. Modern seismologists estimate its magnitude at 8.5 to 9.0 on the Richter scale, with its epicenter in the Atlantic Ocean about 200 kilometers southwest of Cape St. Vincent. The shaking was felt across Europe, from Finland to North Africa.

In Lisbon, the effects were devastating. The dense medieval core of the city, the Baixa district, collapsed almost entirely. Churches, palaces, and the royal library were reduced to rubble. The magnificent Ribeira Palace, which housed the royal court and one of Europe's finest libraries (containing 70,000 volumes, including works by Gutenberg), was destroyed.

But the earthquake was only the beginning. Approximately forty minutes after the initial shock, a tsunami struck the Tagus estuary. Witnesses described three massive waves, estimated at six to twenty meters in height, surging up the river and flooding the lower city. Thousands who had fled to the open spaces along the waterfront to escape collapsing buildings were swept away.

Then came fire. Candles in the churches, cooking fires in kitchens, and overturned lamps ignited a conflagration that burned for five to six days. The fire consumed what the earthquake and tsunami had spared, including irreplaceable archives, artworks, and the contents of the Casa da India, Portugal's trading house with records of two centuries of global commerce.

## The Toll

The death toll remains disputed, but modern estimates suggest between 30,000 and 60,000 people perished in Lisbon alone, out of a pre-earthquake population of roughly 275,000. The tsunami also devastated coastal towns in the Algarve and across the Strait of Gibraltar in Morocco, where the city of Tangier suffered significant damage. Waves were recorded as far away as the Caribbean and the coast of Brazil.

The material destruction was staggering. An estimated 85 percent of Lisbon's buildings were destroyed or severely damaged. Thirty of the city's forty parish churches were ruined. The economic losses were immense, wiping out a significant portion of Portugal's accumulated wealth from centuries of colonial trade.

## Pombal's Response: The Birth of Modern Disaster Management

The man who rose to the occasion was Sebastiao Jose de Carvalho e Melo, later the Marquis of Pombal, who served as the chief minister to King Jose I. When the king asked what should be done, Pombal reportedly replied: "Bury the dead and feed the living."

Pombal's response was a landmark of rational disaster management. He organized firefighting teams, requisitioned food and supplies, and prevented looting through immediate military patrols. He also ordered the disposal of corpses at sea to prevent the outbreak of disease.

Pombal's greatest achievement was the reconstruction of Lisbon. He rejected rebuilding the city along its medieval lines. Instead, he commissioned engineers to design a rational, grid-based city center (the Pombaline Baixa). Buildings were designed with a wooden internal framework (the "Pombaline cage") intended to withstand future seismic activity. This was one of the first known instances of large-scale earthquake-resistant construction.

## The Philosophical Crisis

The earthquake struck at the height of the Enlightenment, a period of optimism and belief in the rationality of the universe. The scale of the disaster, occurring on a religious holiday in a pious city, provoked a profound intellectual crisis across Europe.

Voltaire's *Poeme sur le desastre de Lisbonne* (1756) directly challenged the "optimism" of Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz and Alexander Pope, who argued that "all is for the best in the best of all possible worlds." Voltaire's *Candide* (1759) later satirized this philosophy using the earthquake as a central plot point.

Jean-Jacques Rousseau responded to Voltaire by arguing that the catastrophe's severity was not an act of God but a consequence of human choices—specifically, the dense urban concentration of buildings that had collapsed on their inhabitants. This argument is often seen as the beginning of modern social science and urban studies.

Immanuel Kant wrote three separate essays on the earthquake, attempting to explain it through geological rather than theological causes. His work is considered a foundational contribution to the science of seismology.

## Legacy

The 1755 earthquake transformed Portugal. It ended the country's Baroque period, crippled its economy, and empowered the Marquis of Pombal, who used the reconstruction to centralize state power and implement sweeping reforms. Philosophically, it ended the uncritical optimism of the early Enlightenment and paved the way for more nuanced understandings of the relationship between humanity and nature.
