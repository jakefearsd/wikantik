# Medieval Portugal: Forging a Kingdom (1139-1415)

The story of Portugal begins not with exploration or empire, but with a rebellion. In the early twelfth century, the County of Portugal was a fief within the Kingdom of Leon, governed by Henry of Burgundy, a French nobleman who had come to Iberia to fight in the Reconquista. When Henry died in 1112, his son Afonso Henriques inherited both the county and an ambition that would reshape the Iberian Peninsula.

## The Birth of a Kingdom

Afonso Henriques was not content to remain a vassal. In 1128, he defeated his own mother's forces at the Battle of Sao Mamede near Guimaraes, seizing control of the county. Over the next decade, he waged war against both the Moors to the south and the Kingdom of Leon to the east. The Battle of Ourique in 1139, though its details are wrapped in legend, became the founding myth of Portugal: tradition holds that Afonso saw a vision of Christ before the battle and adopted the five wounds of Christ as the symbols on the Portuguese coat of arms.

By 1143, the Treaty of Zamora recognized Afonso Henriques as King of Portugal, though papal confirmation did not come until 1179. Portugal was now an independent kingdom, one of the first nation-states in Europe.

## The Reconquista Drives South

The new kingdom's most urgent task was the Reconquista, the centuries-long Christian campaign to reclaim the Iberian Peninsula from Muslim rule. Afonso Henriques captured Santarem and, crucially, Lisbon in 1147 with the help of English, Flemish, and German crusaders en route to the Second Crusade. The siege of Lisbon lasted seventeen weeks before the Moorish defenders surrendered.

Subsequent kings continued the push southward. Afonso III (r. 1248-1279) completed the Portuguese Reconquista with the conquest of the Algarve in 1249, making Portugal the first Iberian kingdom to reach its modern continental borders, nearly two and a half centuries before Spain completed its own Reconquista with the fall of Granada in 1492.

## Denis I and the Making of a Nation

If Afonso Henriques was Portugal's warrior-founder, Denis I (r. 1279-1325) was its nation-builder. Known as the "Farmer King" (O Lavrador), Denis transformed Portugal from a frontier warrior state into a functioning medieval kingdom. His achievements were remarkable:

He founded the University of Coimbra in 1290, one of the oldest universities in continuous operation in the world. He made Portuguese, rather than Latin, the official language of government and law. He planted the great pine forest of Leiria, which would later supply timber for the caravels of the Age of Discovery. He negotiated the Treaty of Alcances with Castile in 1297, which established borders that have remained essentially unchanged for over seven centuries, making Portugal's frontier the oldest in Europe.

Denis also reorganized the Order of Christ from the dissolved Knights Templar in 1319, ensuring that the order's wealth and military expertise remained in Portuguese hands. This order would later fund many of the voyages of discovery.

## Feudalism, Portuguese Style

Medieval Portugal was organized along feudal lines, but with distinctive characteristics that set it apart from the rigid hierarchies of France or England. The king granted territories (terras) to nobles (senhores) in exchange for military service and loyalty. Peasants (camponeses) worked the land under obligations of corvee labor, tithes to the Church, and payments of agricultural produce.

However, Portuguese feudalism featured a stronger central monarchy than was typical elsewhere in Europe. The Statute of Almeida of 1290 formalized land rights and obligations, and the Portuguese kings actively regulated feudal relationships, preventing the emergence of overly powerful regional lords. Serfdom was less rigid than in Northern Europe, and peasants had somewhat more mobility.

The Catholic Church was a major landowner and political force, with religious orders like the Order of Santiago controlling vast estates along the frontier. Monasteries such as Alcobaca and Batalha were not just spiritual centers but economic engines, managing agricultural production and trade.

## The 1383-1385 Crisis: A Nation Preserved

The greatest test of medieval Portugal came in 1383, when King Fernando I died without a male heir. His daughter Beatriz was married to King John I of Castile, raising the specter of Portuguese absorption into Castile. The crisis split the country: much of the nobility supported the Castilian claim, while the urban bourgeoisie and common people rallied behind Joao, the illegitimate son of Pedro I and Master of the Order of Aviz.

The showdown came at the Battle of Aljubarrota on August 14, 1385. Despite being heavily outnumbered, the Portuguese forces, commanded by the brilliant general Nuno Alvares Pereira, routed the Castilian army. The victory secured Portuguese independence and brought the House of Aviz to the throne. Joao I became king, and in 1386 he sealed the Treaty of Windsor with England, creating what remains the oldest alliance still in force in the world.

The Battle of Aljubarrota had consequences far beyond the battlefield. The new Aviz dynasty, having come to power with the support of the merchant class rather than the old nobility, was more commercially minded and outward-looking. It was Joao I's sons, particularly Prince Henry (later known as Henry the Navigator), who would launch Portugal's maritime expansion. The magnificent Batalha Monastery, built to commemorate the victory, became one of Portugal's greatest architectural achievements.

## The Medieval Church and Cultural Life

The Church pervaded every aspect of medieval Portuguese life. Cathedrals rose in Lisbon, Coimbra, Evora, and Braga. The Cistercian monastery at Alcobaca, founded in 1153, became one of the wealthiest in Iberia. Monks copied manuscripts, managed farms, and administered justice in their domains.

Religious military orders, particularly the Order of Christ (the reorganized Templars), the Order of Santiago, and the Order of Aviz, controlled significant territory and military resources. These orders would play crucial roles in the coming age of exploration, with the Order of Christ's red cross appearing on the sails of Portuguese caravels.

Portuguese culture in this period absorbed influences from its diverse heritage: Roman administrative traditions, Visigothic customs, and centuries of coexistence with Moorish civilization, which left its mark in architecture, agriculture (irrigation techniques), and language (hundreds of Portuguese words derive from Arabic).

## The Foundations of Empire

By 1415, when Prince Henry led the Portuguese conquest of Ceuta in North Africa, medieval Portugal had built the foundations for everything that would follow. It had a stable monarchy, defined borders, a maritime tradition, a reorganized military-religious order with resources to fund exploration, and a merchant class eager for new trade routes. The medieval kingdom's greatest achievement was perhaps this: a small nation of roughly one million people had created the institutional framework for one of history's most ambitious ventures of discovery.
