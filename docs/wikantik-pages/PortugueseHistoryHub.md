# History of Portugal: Eight Centuries of a Remarkable Nation

Portugal occupies a narrow strip of the Iberian Peninsula, barely 92,000 square kilometers, yet its fingerprints mark every continent on Earth. From the medieval warriors who carved a kingdom out of the Reconquista to the navigators who opened sea routes spanning the globe, from the longest-lived authoritarian regime in Western Europe to the bloodless revolution that ended it with carnations, Portugal's history is a story of outsized ambition, dramatic reversals, and enduring resilience.

This cluster of articles traces that story across eight centuries, from the consolidation of the medieval kingdom around 1200 to the European debt crisis of the 2010s and beyond.

## Why Portugal's History Matters

Consider the paradox: a nation that never exceeded two million people in the Age of Discovery built an empire stretching from Brazil to Macau, from Angola to Timor. Portugal was the first European power to establish a global maritime network, the first to round the Cape of Good Hope, and the last to relinquish its African colonies. Its language is spoken today by over 250 million people across nine countries.

Portugal's history also illuminates broader patterns. The rise and fall of its empire mirrors the story of European colonialism itself. The 1755 Lisbon earthquake reshaped Enlightenment philosophy. The Carnation Revolution of 1974 became a model for peaceful democratic transitions. And Portugal's journey from near-bankruptcy in 2011 to economic recovery offers lessons for our own era.

## The Arc of Portuguese History

**The Medieval Kingdom (1139-1415).** Portugal began as a county within the Kingdom of Leon, winning independence through the military campaigns of Afonso Henriques, its first king. The Reconquista drove the kingdom southward, conquering Lisbon in 1147 and the Algarve by 1249. The Battle of Aljubarrota in 1385 secured sovereignty against Castile, and the Treaty of Windsor with England in 1386 created the oldest alliance still in force today. For more, see [Medieval Portugal](MedievalPortugal).

**The Age of Discovery (1415-1580).** Beginning with the conquest of Ceuta in 1415, Portugal launched the era of European maritime expansion. Prince Henry the Navigator sponsored voyages down the African coast. Bartolomeu Dias rounded the Cape of Good Hope in 1488. Vasco da Gama reached India in 1498. Pedro Alvares Cabral landed in Brazil in 1500. Within a generation, Portuguese traders, missionaries, and soldiers had established footholds from Hormuz to Nagasaki. See [The Portuguese Age of Discovery](PortugueseAgeOfDiscovery).

**The Colonial Empire (1500-1975).** At its height, the Portuguese Empire encompassed Brazil, vast stretches of coastal Africa (Angola, Mozambique, Guinea-Bissau, Cape Verde, Sao Tome), trading posts across India (Goa, Diu, Cochin), Southeast Asia (Malacca, Timor), and China (Macau). The Estado da India governed a commercial network rather than contiguous territory, making Portugal's empire fundamentally different from later land-based empires. See [The Portuguese Colonial Empire](PortugueseColonialEmpire).

**The Lisbon Earthquake (1755).** On All Saints' Day 1755, one of the deadliest earthquakes in recorded history struck Lisbon, followed by a tsunami and fires that destroyed much of the city. The disaster killed between 30,000 and 60,000 people and triggered a philosophical crisis across Europe, challenging the prevailing optimism of the Enlightenment. The Marquis of Pombal's reconstruction effort became a landmark of rational urban planning. See [The Lisbon Earthquake of 1755](LisbonEarthquakeOf1755).

**From Monarchy to Republic (1807-1910).** Napoleon's invasion in 1807 forced the royal court to flee to Brazil, beginning a century of upheaval. Brazil's independence in 1822 stripped Portugal of its most valuable colony. The British Ultimatum of 1890, which forced Portugal to abandon territorial claims in Africa, humiliated the monarchy. The assassination of King Carlos I in 1908 and the revolution of October 5, 1910, ended over seven centuries of royal rule. See [From Monarchy to Republic](PortugueseMonarchyToRepublic).

**Salazar and the Estado Novo (1933-1974).** Antonio de Oliveira Salazar built one of Europe's most enduring authoritarian regimes, ruling Portugal for 36 years through censorship, the PIDE secret police, and a corporatist economic model that kept the country isolated and poor. His refusal to decolonize dragged Portugal into ruinous wars in Angola, Mozambique, and Guinea-Bissau. See [Salazar and the Estado Novo](SalazarAndTheEstadoNovo).

**The Carnation Revolution (1974).** On April 25, 1974, young military officers of the Armed Forces Movement overthrew the regime in a nearly bloodless coup. Civilians placed carnations in the soldiers' rifle barrels, giving the revolution its name. The subsequent two years of revolutionary upheaval, the PREC, transformed Portugal from a colonial dictatorship into a modern democracy. See [The Carnation Revolution](CarnationRevolution).

**Modern Portugal (1976-Present).** Democratic Portugal joined the European Economic Community in 1986, hosted Expo 98, and adopted the euro. The 2010-2014 sovereign debt crisis brought austerity and an international bailout, but Portugal's subsequent recovery, driven by tourism, technology exports, and fiscal discipline, earned it recognition as a model of resilience. See [Modern Portugal](ModernPortugal).
