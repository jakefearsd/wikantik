# From Monarchy to Republic: Portugal's Turbulent Nineteenth Century (1807-1910)

The nineteenth century was the most turbulent in Portuguese history since the 1383 crisis. In little more than a hundred years, Portugal endured a French invasion, the loss of Brazil, civil war, a constitutional revolution, colonial humiliation, regicide, and the overthrow of a monarchy that had endured for over seven centuries. The period transformed Portugal from an absolutist colonial power into a struggling republic searching for its place in modern Europe.

## Napoleon's Invasion and the Flight to Brazil

In November 1807, as Napoleon's armies marched toward Lisbon, the Portuguese royal family made a decision without precedent in European history: the entire court, some 10,000 to 15,000 people, boarded ships and sailed to Brazil. Prince Regent Joao (ruling on behalf of his incapacitated mother, Queen Maria I) chose exile over subjugation.

The French occupied Lisbon under General Junot, but British forces under Arthur Wellesley (the future Duke of Wellington) arrived in 1808 and, with Portuguese support, drove the French out during the Peninsular War. Three French invasions between 1807 and 1811 devastated the Portuguese countryside and accelerated the country's economic decline.

Meanwhile, in Rio de Janeiro, Prince Joao opened Brazilian ports to international trade in 1808, breaking the colonial monopoly that had enriched Lisbon for centuries. He elevated Brazil to a kingdom in 1815, making Portugal part of the "United Kingdom of Portugal, Brazil, and the Algarves." When Joao finally returned to Lisbon in 1821 as King Joao VI, he left his son Pedro behind in Brazil.

## Brazilian Independence and the Liberal Wars

On September 7, 1822, Pedro declared Brazilian independence, becoming Emperor Pedro I of Brazil. For Portugal, this was a catastrophe. Brazil had been the economic engine of the empire since the seventeenth century. Its gold, diamonds, sugar, and coffee had funded the Portuguese state. Overnight, Portugal lost its most valuable possession and the revenues that came with it.

The loss of Brazil coincided with a bitter struggle between liberals and absolutists that would consume Portugal for decades. The Liberal Revolution of 1820 had established Portugal's first constitution in 1822, limiting royal power and creating a parliamentary system. But Queen Maria II's uncle, Miguel, seized the throne in 1828 and attempted to restore absolute rule.

The ensuing Liberal Wars (1828-1834) pitted Miguel's absolutists against liberals led by Pedro (who had abdicated the Brazilian throne in favor of his son). The liberals prevailed, but the wars left Portugal politically unstable and economically exhausted. Between 1834 and 1910, the country cycled through numerous governments, constitutions, and political crises.

## The Regeneration and Its Limits

The period from 1851 to 1890, known as the Regeneration (Regeneracao), brought relative stability and modernization. Governments invested in railways, roads, telegraphs, and public education. Fontes Pereira de Melo, the era's dominant political figure, championed infrastructure development funded by foreign loans.

But modernization came at a price. Portugal's industrial development lagged far behind Northern Europe. The country remained overwhelmingly rural, with high rates of illiteracy (roughly 80 percent in 1890). Economic growth depended heavily on foreign investment and colonial revenues, creating structural vulnerabilities.

## The British Ultimatum of 1890

Portugal's ambitions in Africa collided with British imperial interests in one of the most humiliating episodes in Portuguese history. In the 1880s, Portugal claimed sovereignty over the territory between Angola and Mozambique, envisioning a belt of Portuguese-controlled Africa stretching from the Atlantic to the Indian Ocean, illustrated by the famous "Pink Map" (Mapa Cor-de-Rosa).

Britain had other plans. In January 1890, the British government delivered an ultimatum demanding that Portugal withdraw its forces from the disputed territories (present-day Zimbabwe and Zambia). Faced with the overwhelming power of the British Empire, Portugal capitulated.

The ultimatum provoked a wave of nationalist outrage. The government that accepted it fell. The treaty of Windsor, the oldest alliance in the world, suddenly felt like a collar rather than a bond. Republicans seized on the humiliation as evidence of the monarchy's incompetence, and the event became a rallying point for anti-monarchist sentiment.

## Regicide and Revolution

The final years of the monarchy were marked by crisis. King Carlos I attempted to stabilize the country by appointing Joao Franco as prime minister in 1906 with near-dictatorial powers. This alienated both republicans and monarchist moderates.

On February 1, 1908, as the royal family rode through Lisbon's Terreiro do Paco in an open carriage, assassins opened fire. King Carlos and his eldest son, Crown Prince Luis Filipe, were killed. The younger son, Manuel, survived and was proclaimed King Manuel II at the age of eighteen.

Manuel II's brief reign was unable to halt the monarchy's disintegration. Republican conspirators, supported by elements of the army and navy, launched their revolt on October 3, 1910. After two days of fighting in Lisbon, during which the cruiser Adamastor shelled the royal palace, Manuel II fled to Gibraltar and then to England. On October 5, 1910, the Portuguese Republic was proclaimed from the balcony of Lisbon's City Hall.

The monarchy that had begun with Afonso Henriques in 1139 ended after 771 years. The republic that replaced it would prove even more unstable, lurching through sixteen years of parliamentary chaos before giving way to military dictatorship and, eventually, the authoritarian Estado Novo.

## The Legacy of the Monarchical Collapse

The fall of the Portuguese monarchy was not a single event but the culmination of a century of structural weakness. The loss of Brazil deprived the state of its economic foundation. Chronic political instability prevented coherent governance. The failure to industrialize left Portugal dependent on foreign capital and colonial revenues. And the British Ultimatum shattered the mystique of empire that had sustained the monarchy's legitimacy.

The First Republic (1910-1926) inherited all these problems and solved none of them. In sixteen years, it saw forty-five different governments, a brief participation in World War I that cost 8,000 Portuguese lives, chronic labor unrest, and a military coup in 1926 that ended democratic rule.
