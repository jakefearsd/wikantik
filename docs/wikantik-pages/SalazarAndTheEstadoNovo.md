# Salazar and the Estado Novo: Europe's Quietest Dictatorship (1933-1974)

Antonio de Oliveira Salazar was an unlikely dictator. A former economics professor at the University of Coimbra, bookish and austere, he neither craved the spotlight like Mussolini nor cultivated a personality cult like Stalin. Yet he built one of the most durable authoritarian regimes in twentieth-century Europe, governing Portugal for 36 years with an iron hand wrapped in a velvet glove of bureaucratic tedium.

## The Road to Power

The First Republic (1910-1926) had been a disaster: 45 governments in sixteen years, chronic fiscal crisis, political violence, and a disastrous intervention in World War I. On May 28, 1926, a military coup ended the republic. But the generals who took power had no coherent plan for governance.

Enter Salazar. Appointed finance minister in 1928, he accepted the post on the condition that he would have veto power over all government spending. Within two years, he had balanced the budget for the first time in decades, turning Portugal from a debtor nation into one with a surplus. His reward was power: in 1932, he became prime minister, and in 1933, a new constitution established the Estado Novo (New State), an authoritarian corporate state with Salazar at its center.

## The Architecture of the Estado Novo

The Estado Novo was not a fascist state in the Italian or German mold, though it shared some features. Salazar rejected mass mobilization, populist spectacle, and territorial expansion in Europe. Instead, he built a system based on:

**Corporatism.** The economy was organized into corporations representing different sectors (agriculture, industry, commerce), with the state mediating between employers and workers. Independent trade unions were banned and replaced by state-controlled syndicates. Strikes were illegal.

**Censorship.** The press, radio, and later television were subject to prior censorship. Books, films, and theater were reviewed before publication or performance. The regime promoted a sanitized version of Portuguese history emphasizing the glories of the Age of Discovery and the civilizing mission of the colonial empire.

**The PIDE.** The Policia Internacional e de Defesa do Estado (International and State Defense Police) was the regime's secret police. Modeled partly on the Gestapo, the PIDE monitored dissidents, operated a network of informers, and ran the infamous Tarrafal prison camp in Cape Verde, where political prisoners were held in brutal conditions.

**The Catholic Church.** Salazar, a devout Catholic, restored the Church's privileged position after the anticlerical policies of the Republic. The Concordat of 1940 formalized the relationship between Church and State. Catholic values, particularly the emphasis on family, obedience, and order, permeated the regime's ideology.

**Colonial ideology.** Salazar regarded the overseas territories as integral parts of Portugal, not colonies to be granted independence. The regime's slogan, "Portugal is not a small country," was accompanied by maps showing Portugal's African territories superimposed on the map of Europe, demonstrating that the empire's total area was larger than the European landmass.

## Economic Stagnation and Social Control

Under Salazar, Portugal remained the poorest country in Western Europe. While the rest of the continent industrialized rapidly after World War II, Portugal's economy remained largely agricultural. Per capita income in 1960 was roughly one-third of the European average. Illiteracy rates, though declining, remained high, particularly among women and in rural areas.

The regime maintained social order through a combination of repression and paternalism. The motto "Deus, Patria, Familia" (God, Fatherland, Family) encapsulated its values. Women were largely excluded from public life. Education was limited for the poor. Emigration served as a safety valve: hundreds of thousands of Portuguese left for France, Germany, Brazil, and elsewhere, sending remittances home that helped sustain families the economy could not support.

## The Colonial Wars (1961-1974)

The event that ultimately destroyed the Estado Novo was Salazar's refusal to decolonize. In 1961, India forcibly annexed Goa, Daman, and Diu, ending over four centuries of Portuguese presence. That same year, armed uprisings began in Angola, followed by Guinea-Bissau in 1963 and Mozambique in 1964.

Salazar responded with military force, deploying up to 150,000 troops across three theaters of war simultaneously. For a country of fewer than nine million people, this was an enormous burden. The wars consumed roughly 40 percent of the national budget, drained the economy, and created a generation of disillusioned young officers who had seen the futility of fighting unwinnable colonial conflicts.

The human cost was severe. Approximately 8,000 Portuguese soldiers were killed, with thousands more wounded or psychologically scarred. Casualties among African combatants and civilians were far higher, with estimates running into the hundreds of thousands.

## Salazar's Fall and Caetano's Brief Rule

In September 1968, Salazar suffered a stroke caused by a collapsing deck chair. He was incapacitated but, according to a famous story, was never told he had been replaced, his aides allowing him to believe he was still governing until his death in July 1970.

Marcelo Caetano succeeded Salazar and initially raised hopes of liberalization. He eased some censorship restrictions, allowed limited political debate, and renamed the PIDE (as the DGS). But he continued the colonial wars and proved unable or unwilling to fundamentally reform the regime. The "Marcellist Spring" gave way to renewed repression, and discontent within the military reached a breaking point.

By 1974, Portugal was exhausted: economically stagnant, internationally isolated, fighting three colonial wars it could not win, with a military increasingly unwilling to continue. The stage was set for revolution.
