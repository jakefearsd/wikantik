# Structural Engineering

Structural Engineering is the discipline of creating "bones and muscles" that support human infrastructure. It bridges abstract physics and applied materials science to ensure structures withstand environmental forces without catastrophic failure.

## 1. Core Principles: Loads and Paths
The fundamental goal of a structural engineer is to design a clear, continuous path for forces to travel from the highest point of a structure down into the foundation and the earth.

*   **Dead Loads**: The intrinsic, constant weight of the structure itself (steel beams, concrete slabs).
*   **Live Loads**: Transient forces (people, furniture, vehicles).
*   **Environmental Loads**: Dynamic, often extreme forces like wind shear, seismic activity, and snow accumulation.

## 2. Stress and Strain Analysis
Engineers mathematically model how materials react to these loads.
*   **Stress ($\sigma$)**: Force applied per unit area. 
*   **Strain ($\epsilon$)**: The physical deformation of the material in response to stress.
*   **Young's Modulus ($E$)**: The measure of a material's stiffness. It defines the linear relationship between stress and strain in the elastic region: 

$$
\sigma = E \cdot \epsilon
$$

## 3. Structural Elements
Structures are composed of basic elements, each designed to handle specific types of stress:
*   **Beams**: Horizontal elements primarily resisting bending and shear forces.
*   **Columns**: Vertical elements designed to resist compressive forces.
*   **Trusses**: Frameworks of triangles that convert complex bending forces into pure tension and compression along their members.
