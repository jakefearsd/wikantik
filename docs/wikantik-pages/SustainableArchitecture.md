# Sustainable Architecture

Sustainable architecture seeks to minimize the negative environmental impact of buildings by enhancing efficiency and moderation in the use of materials, energy, and development space. 

## 1. Passive Design Strategies
The most effective sustainable buildings minimize active energy consumption by exploiting local climate conditions.
*   **Solar Orientation**: Aligning the building axis to maximize winter solar heat gain while blocking harsh summer sun (e.g., using strategic overhangs).
*   **Thermal Mass**: Using dense materials (concrete, rammed earth) to absorb heat during the day and slowly release it at night, regulating internal temperature naturally.
*   **Natural Ventilation**: Designing airflow paths to utilize prevailing winds and the stack effect (hot air rising) to cool the building without mechanical air conditioning.

## 2. Embodied Carbon vs. Operational Carbon
Historically, focus was placed on **Operational Carbon** (the energy used to run the building). Modern sustainable architecture also heavily targets **Embodied Carbon**—the greenhouse gas emissions associated with manufacturing, transporting, and assembling the building materials.
*   **Timber and Mass Wood**: Shifting away from concrete and steel (which have massive embodied carbon footprints) toward Cross-Laminated Timber (CLT), which acts as a carbon sink.

## 3. Net-Zero and Circular Systems
A Net-Zero building generates as much energy as it consumes over the course of a year.
*   **Energy Integration**: Extensive use of building-integrated photovoltaics (BIPV) and geothermal heat pumps.
*   **Water Circularity**: Greywater recycling systems and rainwater harvesting to reduce municipal water dependence.
