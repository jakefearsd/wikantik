1. The Evolution of Collaboration: A History of Wikis

  1. Introduction
The Wiki is one of the most transformative tools in the history of the World Wide Web. By allowing users to collaboratively create, edit, and link web pages, wikis democratized information and shifted the internet from a read-only medium to a read-write community. Is [ThisALink](ThisALink)()?

  1. Precursors and Philosophy
Before the first actual wiki, the concepts of linked information were explored by visionaries:
- **Vannevar Bush (1945):** Proposed the "Memex," a hypothetical device to store and link books and records.
- **Douglas Engelbart (1960s):** Demonstrated the "oN-Line System" (NLS), featuring hypertext and collaborative editing.
- **Ted Nelson:** Coined the term "hypertext" and envisioned Project Xanadu.

  1. The First Wiki: WikiWikiWeb (1995)
The history of the wiki proper begins with **Ward Cunningham**. In 1994, while looking for a way to facilitate discussions about software design patterns, he developed the **WikiWikiWeb**.
- **Launch:** March 25, 1995, on c2.com.
- **Name:** "Wiki" is the Hawaiian word for "quick." Cunningham chose it after taking the Wiki Wiki Shuttle at Honolulu International Airport, avoiding the term "quick-web."
- **Key Features:**
    *   Editable by anyone.
    *   Automatic linking (originally using CamelCase, e.g., `ThisIsALink`).
    *   Focus on content over form.

  1. The Rise of Wikipedia (2001)
While early wikis were popular among programmers, the concept went mainstream with **Wikipedia**.
- **Origins:** Started as a feeder project for Nupedia, an expert-written encyclopedia.
- **Founders:** Jimmy Wales and Larry Sanger.
- **Impact:** It proved that a "wisdom of crowds" model could create a comprehensive and largely accurate repository of human knowledge, killing off traditional encyclopedias like Britannica.

  1. Evolution of Wiki Software
- **MediaWiki:** Built for Wikipedia, it became the standard for massive, public wikis.
- **Wikantik:** A feature-rich, Java-based wiki engine (like the one this project is based on), popular for its easy installation and integration with Java environments.
- **Enterprise Wikis:** Tools like **Atlassian Confluence** adapted the wiki concept for corporate knowledge management, adding permissions, refined editors, and integration with business tools.
- **Personal Wikis:** In recent years, tools like **Obsidian**, **Roam Research**, and **Notion** have popularized "personal knowledge management," applying wiki-linking logic to individual note-taking.

  1. How Wikis Changed the Web
1.  **Democratization of Content:** Lowered the barrier to entry for publishing on the web.
2.  **Collaborative Truth:** Shifted the model of authority from a single author to community consensus.
3.  **Hyperlinking Structure:** Popularized the idea of a dense network of internal links rather than a hierarchical tree structure.
4.  **Open Source Documentation:** Became the de-facto standard for documenting software projects (e.g., GitHub Wikis).

  1. Conclusion
From a tool for software patterns to the world's largest encyclopedia, wikis have fundamentally changed how we gather, store, and refine knowledge. They remain a testament to the power of open collaboration.
