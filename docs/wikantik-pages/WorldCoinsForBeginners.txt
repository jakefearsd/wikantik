# World Coins for Beginners

While American coins dominate the US collector market, the vast world of international numismatics offers extraordinary breadth and depth that no single country can match. From ancient Greek silver struck 2,500 years ago to modern commemoratives from over 200 issuing authorities, world coins present collectors with virtually unlimited possibilities. Better yet, many areas of world coin collecting remain significantly undervalued compared to their US counterparts, offering exceptional quality and historical significance at accessible price points.

## Ancient Coins: Where It All Began

The earliest coins were struck in Lydia (modern Turkey) around 600 BCE, and within a century, coinage had spread across the Greek world, Persia, and beyond. Ancient coins are the foundation of numismatics and remain one of its most fascinating areas.

**Greek coins** are prized for their extraordinary artistry. The Athenian tetradrachm ("owl") featuring Athena and her sacred owl is perhaps the most iconic ancient coin, produced from roughly 510-38 BCE. Decent examples can be acquired for $300-800. The silver decadrachm of Syracuse, considered the finest coin of antiquity, features a breathtaking chariot scene and the nymph Arethusa — museum-quality examples bring six figures, but related Syracusan tetradrachms start around $1,000.

**Roman coins** offer an unparalleled historical record. Portrait coins of Roman emperors from Augustus through the fall of the Western Empire in 476 CE provide a visual timeline of nearly 500 years. Common bronze coins of emperors like Constantine I can be purchased for $10-30. Silver denarii of popular emperors (Marcus Aurelius, Trajan, Hadrian) typically range from $50-200 in VF condition. Gold aurei are scarce and start around $2,000-5,000.

**Byzantine coins** continued the Roman tradition with distinctive religious imagery. Gold solidii and their fractions are popular with collectors and available from roughly $300-1,500.

A key advantage of ancient coin collecting is that there are no "date and mint mark" completions to chase. You collect what interests you — a portrait gallery of Roman emperors, Greek city-state issues, or coins depicting specific themes like animals or architecture.

## Medieval and Early Modern European Coins

The medieval period produced remarkable coinage diversity:

- **Hammered coins** — Hand-struck on irregular flans, medieval coins from England, France, the Holy Roman Empire, and Italian city-states have a raw, organic beauty that machine-made coins lack. English hammered silver pennies of Edward I (1272-1307) are available for $30-100.
- **Gold florins and ducats** — The Florentine gold florin (from 1252) and Venetian gold ducat (from 1284) became the dominant trade coins of medieval Europe. Genuine examples start around $500-800.
- **Spanish colonial coins** — The famous "pieces of eight" (8 reales) minted throughout Spain's American colonies are deeply connected to the age of exploration and piracy. Cob-style coins — crudely struck on irregular blanks — are particularly evocative and available from $100-500 depending on condition and mint.
- **Thalers** — Large silver coins first minted in Bohemia in 1519, thalers became the standard large silver denomination across Central Europe. The word "dollar" derives from "thaler." Maria Theresa thalers, first struck in 1741, remained in production and circulation well into the 20th century.

## Asian Coins

**Chinese cash coins** — Round bronze coins with square center holes — were produced for over two thousand years, from the Qin Dynasty (221 BCE) through the early Republic. Common examples cost just $1-5, making them an extremely accessible entry point to Asian numismatics. Rarer dynasty issues and large-format cash can bring substantial premiums.

**Japanese coins** include the distinctive oval gold koban and smaller bu coins of the Tokugawa shogunate (1601-1868), as well as the beautifully designed modern yen coinage. Meiji-era (1868-1912) silver coins are popular with collectors and often available in attractive grades.

**Indian coins** span an incredible range from ancient punch-marked silver karshapanas through Mughal gold mohurs to British India rupees. The Mughal coinage, featuring beautiful calligraphic Arabic script, is particularly admired. Gold mohurs of Akbar and Jahangir are highly prized, while silver rupees of common Mughal rulers are accessible at $20-100.

## Collecting Approaches for World Coins

**By country** — Choose a country whose history interests you and explore its numismatic heritage. British coins from the hammered medieval period through modern decimals offer extraordinary variety. French coins from the ancien regime through the Revolution, Napoleon, and the Republic tell the dramatic story of France. German states coinage prior to unification in 1871 encompasses hundreds of issuing authorities.

**By era** — Focus on a historical period across multiple countries. Napoleonic-era coinage from France and its satellite states, or World War II-era coins showing wartime metal substitutions and emergency issues, make compelling thematic collections.

**By denomination type** — Collect crown-size silver coins (roughly 38-40mm diameter) from around the world. British crowns, French 5 francs, German 5 marks, and their equivalents from dozens of countries share a similar size and silver content while showcasing diverse designs.

**By theme** — Animals, ships, coats of arms, portraits of monarchs, maps, or any subject that appeals to you. Modern commemorative coins from countries like Australia, Canada, and various Pacific island nations offer stunning designs in precious metals.

## Authentication Challenges

World coins present unique authentication challenges:

- **Restrikes** — Many countries officially restruck earlier coin designs, sometimes using original dies. Austrian restrike thalers, Hungarian restrike ducats, and others can confuse collectors. These are genuine government products but are worth less than original strikes.
- **Tourist copies** — In countries with significant ancient coin heritage (Greece, Turkey, Egypt), tourist-market reproductions are common. Most are obviously fake to experienced eyes, but some are better.
- **Chinese counterfeits** — As with US coins, sophisticated counterfeits from China affect the world coin market. Gold coins, crown-size silver, and ancient coins are primary targets.
- **Attribution complexity** — Many world coins lack the comprehensive cataloging that US coins enjoy. The Standard Catalog of World Coins (Krause-Mishler) is the essential multi-volume reference, but even it has gaps for certain series and periods.

For significant purchases, NGC is the preferred grading service for world and ancient coins, offering certification with detailed attribution.

## Getting Started with World Coins

Practical steps for entering world coin collecting:

1. **Start with what interests you historically.** If you love Roman history, start with Roman coins. If the Age of Sail captivates you, explore Spanish colonial coinage.
2. **Buy the book before the coin.** Invest in the relevant Krause catalog or specialized reference for your area of interest.
3. **Start affordable.** Ancient Roman bronzes, Chinese cash coins, and circulated 19th-20th century silver from most countries offer genuine historical artifacts at modest prices.
4. **Attend shows with world coin dealers.** Major shows always have dealers specializing in world coins, and many are happy to educate interested buyers.
5. **Join specialized groups.** Organizations like the Ancient Numismatic Society, the Oriental Numismatic Society, and country-specific collector groups provide community and expertise.

World coin collecting is a journey without end. The sheer number of countries, periods, and denominations means you will always find something new to discover and pursue. The field rewards curiosity and a love of history above all else.
